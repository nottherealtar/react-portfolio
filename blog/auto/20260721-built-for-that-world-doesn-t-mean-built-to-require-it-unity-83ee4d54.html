In a pleasant shock for people who've been keeping up with Unity's doings these past couple of years, it doesn't appear to ruin anything. Aside from more routine promises of "speedier workflows" and tastier graffix, the company are promising a more "open" approach whereby collaborators using third-party tools can contribute more easily to Unity projects, without using the full Unity editor. Still, the real headline 'innovation' appears to be that most boring and blessed of things: straightforward compatibility with videogame projects using the previous incarnation of Unity's gadgetry. "All projects, skills, and code will carry forward smoothly into the next generation," the company promised in an announcement from Unite Seoul, via Automation . Picture Moses coming down the mountain with a stone tablet that reads "fear not, impoverished goblin coders who are four years into their projects - you won't have to f**king…

If you haven't been keeping up with Unity, they put themselves in the doghouse in 2023 by proposing to charge game developers a fee everytime somebody installs a Unity game . Game developers found the concept displeasing. Unity rowed back the plans a year later, having comprehensively nuked their reputation for being a friend of smaller outfits.

All this took place against a backdrop of mass layoffs , as Unity joined the ranks of companies looking to "right the ship" following the Covid lockdown boom. It's due to enter early beta testing in December 2026, with the final release taking place sometime in early 2027. It will adopt CoreCLR as its default scripting backend, which will apparently make for faster Play Mode startup times (Play Mode is a preview feature that lets devs see how a WIP game would appear to a regular player) and shader compilation.

It will add a new global illumination system, Surface Cache GI, which is designed for more believable lighting in worlds that move around a lot. As ever, I apologise to any more knowledgeable readers for my blundering layperson summaries. There's a new command line interface and public application programming interface which apparently let external developers "validate assets, push builds, and collaborate from their own tools".

As explained by senior vice president of product Matt Smith, an artist working in Blender could be sent a "deep link" that carries "the full project, scene, asset, and revision context" needed to make a fix, without being given proper editor access. The artist could then make that fix in a browser dashboard, and check it against in-game lighting an animation using a scene preview feature, before handing it off to project engineers to be checked over and added to a build of the game.
