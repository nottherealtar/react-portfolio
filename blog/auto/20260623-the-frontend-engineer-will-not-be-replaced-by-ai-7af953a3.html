I was staring at a terminal, sweat beading on my forehead, waiting for a deployment to finish. Three forms, a validation step, and a confirmation screen. And in the morning, thousands of people would wake up, open this app, and try to feed their families.

If the coupon didn't apply correctly, someone would overpay. Someone might not be able to afford what they needed that week. It's the difference between someone eating dinner and someone going hungry.

Between a business staying open and closing. Between a delivery arriving on time or a child missing their school lunch. Five years later, I've shipped hundreds of features.

But that 2 AM deployment, that moment of realizing my code mattered, is the fire that's kept me going. And now I'm watching the news tell an entire generation of aspiring engineers, "AI is coming for your job. When I first saw an AI generate a functional React component from a sentence, my stomach dropped.

I'd spent years learning to write that exact thing. It doesn't know why the button is orange instead of blue. It doesn't know that the loading state needs to appear for exactly 300 milliseconds, not 200, not 400, because user testing showed that 300ms is the sweet spot between perceived speed and perceived effort.

It doesn't know that the disabled submit button should show a tooltip explaining why it's disabled. It doesn't know that the error message should say, "This email is already registered. Let us help you reset it" instead of "Error: duplicate entry." AI doesn't know these things because these things are not in the training data.

They're in the conversations with product managers and customer support agents and actual human beings who use what you build. I've built admin dashboards that give businesses superpowers, the ability to see, understand, and act on their operations in ways they never could before.
